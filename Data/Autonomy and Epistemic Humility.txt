Max Stirner was a German philosopher known for his radical egoism and individualism, primarily articulated in his book "The Ego and Its Own" (also translated as "The Unique and Its Property"). His thought has been influential on post-left anarchy and individualist anarchism. Here's a basic outline of Stirner's philosophy:
Egoism and the Unique
: Stirner argued that the individual ("the Unique") is the only reality, and rejected any concepts or ideas that would place restrictions on the individual, such as morality, religion, or the state. This is the basis of his egoism. For Stirner, one's own interests should always be the primary motivation for action.
Rejection of Fixed Ideas or "Spooks"
: Stirner criticized ideologies that he considered "spooks" or illusions, including religion, nationalism, and even notions like human rights or morality. These are seen as artificial constructs that individuals internalize, allowing these ideas to control them. Stirner argued for the dismissal of these abstractions in favor of the concrete individual.
The Creative Nothing
: Stirner's concept of the "creative nothing" refers to the individual's potential for self-creation and transformation. This concept emphasizes the boundless potential of the individual, untethered from any predefined roles or identities.
Property and Ownership
: Stirner's view of property was unconventional. He saw property as a matter of might: if an individual has the power to claim something and defend it, then it is their property. This isn't to be confused with endorsement of theft or force; rather, it's an existential claim about personal autonomy.
Union of Egoists
: Stirner proposed the idea of a "union of egoists", a voluntary and non-hierarchical association of individuals who cooperate out of self-interest. This stands in contrast to the state or society, which Stirner saw as oppressive entities imposing their will on the individual.
Critique of Ideologies
: Stirner critiqued both liberal and socialist ideologies of his time, including the ideas of his contemporaries such as Ludwig Feuerbach and Karl Marx. He argued that these ideologies simply replaced one absolute (God, for example) with another (such as Humanity or the Proletariat), and therefore continued to limit the individual.
Stirner's philosophy has been seen as nihilistic, individualistic, or anarchist, but it defies easy categorization. It's important to note that his radical egoism isn't about selfishness in the conventional sense, but about the rejection of any external limitations on the individual. His ideas have been influential in a variety of fields, from philosophy and political theory to psychology and the arts.

Francis Bacon, a philosopher from the 16th and 17th century, and Max Stirner, from the 19th century, both critiqued prevailing ideologies and cognitive distortions of their respective times, but they did so in distinct ways that reflected their unique philosophical perspectives.
Bacon is well-known for his concept of the "Four Idols," which he presented in his work "Novum Organum" as obstacles to clear thinking and true understanding:
Idols of the Tribe
: These are common errors of human nature, like seeking patterns where none exist or favoring information that confirms our pre-existing beliefs (confirmation bias).
Idols of the Cave
: These are personal biases due to one's own experiences and preferences.
Idols of the Marketplace
: These are confusions and deceptions created by language and communication, including the use of ambiguous or ill-defined words.
Idols of the Theater
: These are distortions caused by adherence to philosophical or theological dogmas, much like uncritically accepting a script.
Bacon's aim was to free scientific inquiry from these idols, promoting empirical observation and inductive reasoning as the best ways to understand the natural world.
Stirner, on the other hand, introduced the concept of "spooks" or "ghosts" in his work "The Ego and Its Own". These are abstract concepts like God, the state, or morality, which he saw as artificial constructs that hold power over individuals, limiting their autonomy and self-interest. Stirner argued that individuals should free themselves from these spooks to fully realize their own desires and potential.
Comparing the two, Bacon's idols are obstacles to clear and unbiased understanding of the natural world, while Stirner's spooks are ideologies or concepts that limit individual freedom and self-interest. Both philosophers advocate for critical examination of received ideas, but their focus differs: Bacon emphasizes empirical observation and inductive reasoning, while Stirner emphasizes individual autonomy and self-interest.

The distinction between the individual and society can indeed be seen as a false dichotomy, particularly from certain theoretical perspectives. Here's why:
Interconnectedness
: Individuals and societies are not separate entities but are deeply intertwined. Societies are made up of individuals, and individuals, in turn, are shaped by the societies in which they live. Social norms, cultural practices, languages, institutions - all these aspects of society shape an individual's beliefs, behaviors, and identity. Simultaneously, societies evolve and change as individuals interact with each other and make collective decisions.
Social Constructionism
: From a social constructionist perspective, the self is not a pre-existing entity but is constructed through social interactions. Our identities are formed through our relationships with others and our engagement with social and cultural practices. So, the boundary between the individual and society is not clear-cut.
Societal Influence on Individual Behavior
: From a sociological perspective, an individual's behavior is often influenced by social structures and cultural norms. Theories like Durkheim's concept of social facts or Bourdieu's concept of habitus emphasize how society shapes individual actions and dispositions.
Individual Influence on Society
: On the other hand, individuals can effect societal change through their actions, innovations, or resistance to existing norms and structures. This perspective is evident in theories of social change and social movements.
However, while the distinction between individual and society can be seen as a false dichotomy, it can also be a useful heuristic for understanding certain phenomena. For instance, some psychological theories focus on individual cognition and behavior, while sociological theories often focus on social structures and processes. Both levels of analysis can provide valuable insights.
In conclusion, the relationship between the individual and society is complex and dialectical. They are not separate entities but are deeply interconnected, with individuals shaping society and society shaping individuals. However, the extent and nature of this interconnection can vary depending on the theoretical perspective one adopts.

Flatworms can learn in a variety of behavioral paradigms and are a unique model system in which regeneration and memory can be studied in the same animal. Bioelectrical controls of vertebrate appendage regeneration

���������

Inducing the host to form structures it already knows how to make is a very desirable property for regenerative medicine approaches since avoids the inevitable complexity explosion of having to micromanage (directly bioengineer) the creation of complex organs and appendages. Bioelectrical, non-local controls of regenerative polarity in planaria

������

Planarian flatworms have an impressive capacity for regeneration. Upon cutting, these organisms are able to regenerate the head and tail at their appropriate locations. What mechanisms determine the polarity and allow tissue re-patterning to take place? Consider: after bisection, cells on one side of the cut (in the head fragment's posterior end) will form a tail, while cells which were their immediate neighbors before the cut will make a head (the tail fragment's anterior half). Our data suggest that the mechanism by which blastema cells polls the rest of the host (to determine where the wound is located and what other tissues already exist in the fragment and thus don't need to be recreated) is mediated by physiological signals passing through nerves and long-range gap junctional paths. More broadly, this work identifies a molecular glimpse of how the "target morphology" of an animal (the form towards which regeneration regulates) can be permanently reset, and reveals that a drastic change in body structure and behavior can be maintained across a complex metazoan's organism's normal mode of reproduction without any change in DNA sequence.

Left-Right Asymmetry

������

The vertebrate body plan is basically bilaterally-symmetrical; however, consistent and well-conserved asymmetries of the brain and visceral organs are superimposed upon the fundamental structure. Asymmetries in the left-right axis present a number of deep puzzles which link evolutionary biology, clinical medicine, biochemistry, embryology, cognitive science, and perhaps even quantum parity violations. Strikingly, it is now known that even single mammalian cells in culture maintain a consistent left-right axis. We are working to understand the mechanisms by which the embryo aligns the left-right axis with respect to the other two axes, and imposes this spatial information on macroscopic cell fields prior to the morphogenesis of the asymmetric organs. In contrast to popular models of asymmetry initiation by extracellular fluid flow during gastrulation, our lab studies much earlier, intracellular events that break symmetry and establish consistent asymmetry as a form of planar cell polarity, using physiological mechanisms to amplify cytoskeletal chirality across cell fields.

Gap Junctions in Pattern Formation

While asymmetrically expressed genes have been identified in several vertebrate systems, many critical questions remain about how cellular polarity is synchronized and amplified across embryonic fields to allow cells to ascertain their position with respect to the midline. We identified a dependence of asymmetric gene expression on early communication between left and right sides in the chick and frog. For example, expression of left-sided markers depends on events occurring on the right side, during very early stages, suggesting that the two sides need to coordinate their decision with respect to the L-R identity of each. One mechanism for communicating between cells and tissues involves gap junctions: multimers of connexin proteins form channels between cells and pass small molecules, subject to complex regulation by various signals. Using misexpression of Connexin proteins and their mutants to disrupt and induce long-range gap junctional paths in early chick and frog embryos, we showed that gap junctions are crucially involved in L-R patterning in early embryos of Xenopus and chick. The data suggest the presence of a unidirectional circumferential flow of small molecules through gap junctions across the whole embryonic field during blastula/early gastrula stages. Our research focuses on understanding the mechanisms upstream and downstream of specific gap junction communications (GJC) in embryos, as they relate to pattern formation and growth control, and on identifying the small molecule morphogens that traverse junctions. More broadly, we focus on gap junctions as a bioelectric patterning element that sets up domains of isopotential cell fields during morphogenesis. More recently, we have identified roles for the electrical synapses known as gap junctions in mediating bioelectric regulation of tumorigenesis, brain patterning, and neural pathfinding.

Bioelectric Aspects of Very Early Left-Right Patterning

L-R asymmetry can only be derived from gap-junctional movement of determinants that is directionally biased. In frog and chick embryos, we have identified a set of four ion transporters which reliably establish a voltage gradient along the midline. This gradient is required for normal asymmetry, and our current quantitative models suggest that it is sufficient to redistribute small molecule morphogens from left to right through the gap junctional paths. These transporters establish a battery across the midline, and in Xenopus, this occurs by the second cell cleavage. Molecular localization of ion channel/pump proteins, and direct detection of asymmetric ion flows (H+ and K+ ions) reveal that these embryos know their left from their right within about 2 hours of fertilization.
