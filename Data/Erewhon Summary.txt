Its title is the word "nowhere" spelled backwards with the 'w' and 'h' transposed.

The book is a satire that explores many aspects of Victorian society through an account of the strange customs of the people in the fictional country of Erewhon.The novel begins with the narrator, an Englishman known only as Higgs, travelling to an unidentified colony reminiscent of New Zealand, where he works as a sheep farmer.

After several years, he embarks on an exploratory journey into the mountains and discovers a hidden civilization known as Erewhon (anagram of "nowhere").Erewhon appears utopian at first, but it's soon revealed that its society is riddled with inconsistencies and paradoxes.

For example, in Erewhon, illness is considered a crime and is punishable, while criminal offenses are treated as diseases to be cured.

The Erewhonians also fear machines and believe they could potentially surpass humans in evolution, so they've banned them entirely - a concept which was remarkably prescient given that Butler wrote this before the age of modern computers.The society's religious beliefs and practices also deviate sharply from the norms familiar to the narrator.

They have a peculiar approach towards religion, viewing it as a set of unknowable mysteries, and they actively discourage questioning or seeking the truth about their gods.

This forms the basis of Butler's critique of institutional religion.During his stay in Erewhon, Higgs falls in love with a local woman, Yram, who he later discovers is the daughter of the king.

He's eventually arrested for possessing a watch (a forbidden machine), but manages to escape back to his home country with the aid of Yram.

The book ends with Higgs planning a return expedition to Erewhon.The novel is more than just a story; it is a platform for Butler's criticisms of Victorian society, including its hypocrisy, its rigid social conventions, its flawed education and legal systems, and its dubious religious practices.
