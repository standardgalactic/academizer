𝘏𝘢𝘷𝘦 𝘺𝘰𝘶 𝘩𝘦𝘢𝘳𝘥 𝘰𝘧 𝘛𝘩𝘦 𝘈𝘯𝘪𝘮𝘢𝘭 𝘔𝘪𝘯𝘥 (1908)?

Yes, "The Animal Mind" is a seminal work by psychologist Margaret Floy Washburn, published in 1908. It is considered one of the first books in the field of comparative psychology, which is the scientific study of animal behavior and cognition. Washburn's work was groundbreaking at the time because it compiled and analyzed existing research on the mental processes of non-human animals, arguing that animals are capable of a wide range of mental processes that were previously attributed only to humans.

"The Animal Mind" discusses topics such as animal consciousness, learning, perception, and decision-making. Washburn utilized introspection (examining one's own conscious thoughts and feelings) cautiously as a method to infer the presence of consciousness in animals, relying instead on observable behaviors to make her case. She was a pioneering figure in arguing for the importance of behavior as evidence for mental activity in animals, laying the groundwork for future research in animal cognition and behaviorism.

Margaret Floy Washburn was the first woman to earn a Ph.D. in psychology (1894) and the second woman to serve as the president of the American Psychological Association (1921). Her contributions, particularly "The Animal Mind," have had a lasting impact on psychology, encouraging further research into understanding the minds of non-human animals and challenging the anthropocentric view of intelligence and consciousness.

Korzybski's General Semantics encourages the development of what he termed "consciousness of abstracting," which is the awareness of how our perceptions, language, and thinking processes abstract and thus simplify reality.
