¿Ignorar?
now
Thales (ca. 625-545 B.C.)
Begins to replace supernatural explanations of the universe with naturalistic ones; encourages criticism and improvement of his teachings
now
Heraclitus (ca. 540-480 B.C.)
Observes that everything in the empirical world is in a constant state of flux and therefore can never be known with certainty.
now
Protagoras (ca. 485-415 B.C.)
Argues that "truth" can only be understood in terms of an individual's perceptions.
now
Hippocrates (ca. 460-377 B.C.)
Argues that both mental and physical disorders have natural causes; a physician's primary task is to facilitate the body's natural healing ability.
now
Democritus (ca. 460-370 B.C.)
Proposes a completely materialistic universe wherein everything consists of atoms.
now
Antisthenes (ca. 445-365 B.C.)
Preaches Cynicism or a back-to-nature philosophy whereby life is lived free from wants, passions, and the conventions of society.
now
Plato (ca. 427-347 B.C.)
Postulates a dualistic universe consisting of abstract forms and matter. Because only the forms are changeless they alone can be known with certainty.
now
Aristotle (384-322 B.C.)
Argues that an understanding of nature must begin with its direct examination. Everything in nature has an inherent purpose that seeks to manifest itself.
now
Pyrrho of Elis (ca. 365-275 B.C.)
Observes that because the arguments for or against any belief are equally valid the only reasonable position is Skepticism or the withholding of belief in anything
now
Epicurus of Samos (ca. 341-270 B.C.)
Encourages living a simple life of moderation and one that is free of superstition.
now
Zeno of Citium (ca. 333-262 B.C.)
Founds the philosophy of Stoicism with his beliefs that nature is governed by a divine plan and that living in accordance with that plan with courage and dignity is the ultimate
now
Philo (ca. 25 B.c.-50 A.D.)
A Neoplatonist, preaches that God will reveal knowledge to souls properly prepared to receive it.
now
Galen (ca. 130-200)
Perpetuates the naturalistic medicine of such Greeks as Hippocrates into the Roman empire, extends the theory of four humors into a rudimentary theory of personality.
now
Constantine (ca. 280-337)
Signs the Edict of Milan in 313, making Christianity a tolerated religion in the Roman empire
now
Augustine (354-430)
Combines Stoicism Neoplatonism and Hebrew religion into a powerful Christian teleology according to which evil exists because people choose it, and God can be experienced personally through introspection
now
Avicenna (980-1037)
An Arabic philosopher/physician, applies Aristotelian philosophy to a wide range of topics and attempts to make it compatible with Muslim theology.
now
Anselm (ca. 1033-1109)
Adds reason to the ways of knowing God with his ontological argument for the existence of God.
now
Peter Lombard (ca. 1095-1160)
Argues that God can be known through the Scriptures, through reason, or by studying nature.
now
Maimonides (1135-1204)
A Jewish scholar and physician attempts to reconcile Judaism and Aristotelian philosophy
now
Thomas Aquinas (1225-1274)
Succeeds in making Aristotelian philosophy the basis of Christian theology.
now
William of Occam (ca. 1290-1350)
Argues that explanations should always be as parsimonious as possible. In the realist-nominalist debate, he sides with the nominalists, thereby encouraging Empiricism
now
Francesco Petrarch (1304-1374)
Sometimes considered the father of the Renaissance argues for the full exploration and manifestation of human potential.
now
Desiderius Erasmus (1466-1536)
Opposes fanaticism, religious ritual, and superstition. Argues that fools are better off accordance with than "wise" persons because fools live in their true feelings.
now
Nicolaus Copernicus (1473-1543)
Publishes De Revolutionibus Orbium Coelestim (The Revolutions of the Heavenly Spheres in 1543, in which he proposes the heliocentric theory of the solar system.)
now
Cornelius Agrippa (1486-1535)
The frst physician to urge that witch-hunts be stopped because those accused of being witches, or of being bewitched , are actually mentally disturbed.
now
Philippus Paracelsus (1493-1541)
Among the first physicians to suggest that the unusual behavior displayed by "witches" and those bewitched had natural rather than supernatural origins,
now
Martin Luther (1483-1546)
Nails his Ninety-Five Theses on the door of Wittenberg Cathedral thereby beginning the Reformation (1517).
now
Michel de Montaigne (1533-1592)
Reintroduces radical Skepticism into the late Renaissance
now
Francis Bacon (1561-1626)
Argues for an inductive science based on the direct examination of nature and the careful generalization of those observations. Theory must be avoided because it biases observations.
now
Galileo (1564-1642)
Through experimentation finds many previously held beliefs about nature to be false. This brings him into conflict with the Church because many of these fallacies were part of church dogma. Denies that cognitive experience can be studied scientifcally thereby inhibiting the development of experimental psychology.
now
Thomas Hobbes (1588-1679)
Argues that all knowledge is derived from sensory experience, and that all human motivation and emotions are reducible to hedonism. Governments are formed to protect people from each other..
now
Pierre Gassendi (1592-1655)
Like Hobbes, says humans consist of nothing but matter, thus rejecting dualism in favor of physical monism.
now
René Descartes (1596-1650)
Uses the method of doubt to confirm the validity of his subjective experiences. Concludes that several important ideas are innate and that humans consist of of a a physical body and a nonphysical mind.
now
Giordano Bruno (1548-1600)
is burned at the stake for heresy.
now
John Locke (1632-1704)
Forcefully argues against the existence of innate ideas, saying instead that all ideas are derived from experience. Once they exist, however, ideas can be rearranged in countless ways.
now
Baruch Spinoza (1632-1677)
Equates God and nature, and claims mind and matter are inseparable All things in nature, including humans are governed by natural law and thus free will does not exist
now
Isaac Newton (1642-1727)
Describes the universe as a complex, lawful machine governed by the law of gravitation and precisely describable in mathematical terms.
now
Gottfried Wilhelm von Leibniz (1646-1716)
Argues that experience can only actualize ideas that already exist within us. Everything in nature consists of monads that vary in their ability to think clearly.
now
George Berkeley (1685-1753)
Denies the existence of a material world, saying instead that only perceptions (ideas) exist. Thus, "to be is to be perceived."
now
David Hartley (1705-1757)
Supplements associationism with speculations about neurophysiology
now
Julien de La Mettrie (1709-1751)
Publishes L'Homme Machine (Man a Machine) in 1748, which embraces physical monism and that the differences between humans and animals is quantitative, not qualitative.
now
Thomas Reid (1710-1796)
Argues that we can assume physical reality is as we perceive it because it makes common sense to do so. Innate faculties of the mind facilitate the accurate perception of the physical world.
now
David Hume (1711-1776)
Argues that humans can never know the physical world with certainty because all we ever experience are the ideas created by that world.
now
Jean-Jacques Rousseau (1712-1778)
Initiates the modern romantic movement by claiming that human feelings are better guides for living than rational deliberations.
now
Étienne Bonnot de Condillac (1715-1780)
Demonstrates that a statue capable of only sensation, memory, and the feelings of pleasure and pain can display all human faculties and abilities
now
Immanuel Kant (1724-1804)
Argues that external reality can never be known because conscious experience always results from the interaction between sensory experience and the innate categories of thought.
now
Franz Anton Mesmer (1734-1815)
Claims to cure disorders by redistributing animal magnetism in his patients.
now
Jean Lamarck (1744-1829)
Publishes his Philosophie Zoologique in 1809, in which he elaborates his theory of the inheritance of acquired characteristics.
now
Benjamin Rush (1745-1813)
Sometimes referred to as the frst psychiatrist in the United States, argues against slavery, capital and public punishment, and the inhumane treatment of prisoners and the mentally ill.
now
Philippe Pinel (1745-1826)
Is appointed director of the Bicêtre Asylum in 1793 and begins releasing inmates from their chains. Segregates different types of patients, encourages occupational therapy, bans punishment.
now
Franz Joseph Gall (1758-1828)
Claims that the extent to which one possesses various faculties can be determined by examining the bumps and depressions on the skull (phrenology).
now
Georg Wilhelm Friedrich Hegel (1770-1831)
Views the universe as an interrelated whole that he called The Absolute, and argues that nothing can be understood except in its relationship to The Absolute.
now
James Mill (1773-1836)
Argues that any idea, no matter how complex, can be understood in terms of the simple ideas it comprises.
now
Johann Friedrich Herbart (1776-1841)
Argues that ideas compete for conscious expression; successful ideas become part of the apperceptive mass, unsuccessful ideas remain unconscious.
now
Arthur Schopenhauer (1788-1860)
Argues that the only relief from the unending cycle of needs and need satisfaction comes from the sublimation, denial, or repression of those needs.
now
Pierre Flourens (1794-1867)
Performs experiments that demonstrate the cerebral cortex functions as an interrelated whole and is not divided into discrete faculties as the phrenologists had claimed.
now
Ernst Heinrich Weber (1795-1878)
Observes that "just noticeable differences" in variable stimuli correspond to a constant fraction of a standard stimulus.
now
Auguste Comte (1798-1857)
Promotes positivistic philosophy according to which only publicly observed phenomena can be known with certainty; metaphysical speculation is to be actively avoided.
now
Johannes Müller (1801-1858)
Formulates the doctrines of specific nerve energies and adequate stimulation
now
Gustav Theodor Fechner (1801-1887)
By noting that for sensations to rise arithmetically the magnitude of the physical stimulus must rise geometrically creates the field of psychophysics.
now
Dorothea Lynde Dix (1802-1887)
Campaigning for over 40 years, vastly improves the plight of the mentally ill in the U.S. and Europe.
now
John Stuart Mill (1806-1873)
Argues that simple ideas can combine and form ideas different from the simple ideas that they comprise (mental chemistry). Believes that a science of psychology is possible, which would describe human nature in general, and that the discipline of ethology would explain
now
Charles Darwin (1809-1882)
Publishes On the Origin of the Species by Means of Natural Selection in 1859, in which he describes how animals with adaptive features survive and reproduce and those without such features do not.
now
Soren Kierkegaand (1813-1855)
Urges a return to the personal, introspective religion described by Augustine. Truth must be understood in terms of what is privately and emotioal embraced by an individual; thus "truth is subjectivity."
now
Alexander Bain (1818-1903)
Seeks the biological correlates of cognition and behavior. His analysis of voluntary behavior resembles the later analyses of Thorndike and Skinner. Marks the transition between philosophical and scientific psychology. Founds the journal Mind in 1876.
now
Herbert Spencer (1820-1903)
Erroneously generalizes Darwinian principles to societies, thus creating social Darwinism.
now
Hermann von Helmholtz (1821-1894)
Promotes positivist medicine, measures the rate of nerve conduction, and makes significant contributions to an understanding of color vision, hearing, and perception in general.
now
Francis Galton (1822-1911)
Publishes Hereditary Genius: An Inquiry into its Laws and Consequences in 1869, in which he argues that intelligence is largely inherited. Introduces the "questionnaire".
now
Paul Broca (1824-1880)
Conclusively demonstrates that an area on the left hemisphere of the cortex is specialized for speech. Also, incorrectly concludes that brain size and intelligence are positively correlated
now
Jean-Martin Charcot (1825-1893)
Speculates that in individuals predisposed to hysteria, trauma may cause certain ideas to become dissociated from consciousness and grow strong enough to cause the symptoms associated with hysteria.
now
Ivan M. Sechenov (1829-1905)
Founds Russian objective Psychology with the publication of Reflexes of the Brain in 1863.
now
Wilhelm Maximilian Wundt (1832-1920)
Founds voluntarism, psychology's first school. This school was very much in the rationalistic tradition with its emphasis on will and purpose.
now
Ewald Hering (1834-1918)
Offers a nativistic explanation of space perception and color vision.
now
Ernst Mach (1838-1916)
Promotes a positivistic philosophy according to which sensations are all that humans can be certain of; therefore scientists must determine the relation-sensations and avoid metaphysical explanations.
now
Franz Clemens Brentano (1838-1917)
Observes that mental acts always refer to (intend) events outside of themselves. With his emphases on mental acts and intentionality, creates the field of act psychology
now
Hippolite Bernheim (1840-1919)
Under the influence of Auguste Ambroise Liébeault (1823-1904) becomes the major spokesperson of the Nancy school of hypnosis claimed that all people can be hypnotized because all people which are suggestible.
now
William James (1842-1910)
Publishes The Principles of Psychology in 1890. This text is often cited as marking the beginning of the school of functionalism
now
Friedrich Wilhelm Nietzsche (1844-1900)
Views life as a struggle between the Apollonian (rational) and the Dionysian (irrational) aspects of human nature and an individual's personality as an artistic blending of these two aspects
now
Granville S. Hall (1844-1924)
Founds the APA in 1892 and serves as its first president. Makes signifcant contributions to developmental psychology but opposes the coeducation of adolescents and young adults.
now
Christine Ladd-Franklin (1847-1930)
Offers a theory of color vision based on evolutionary theory.
now
George John Romanes (1848-1894)
Does early work on comparative psychology but his conclusions are supported only by anecdotal evidence.
now
Ivan Petrovitch Pavlov (1849-1936)
Receives the 1904 Nobel Prize for his research on digestion, during which he discovers the conditioned reflex. Believes the conditioned reflex provides an objective, physiological explanation for what psychologists and philosophers had called associationism
now
Hermann Ebbinghaus (1850-1909)
Publishes On Memory: An Investigation in Experimental Psychology in 1885, marking the first time learning and memory are studied experimentally..
now
Hans Vaihinger (1852-1933)
Argues that acting "as if" certain fallacious concepts are true is essential for societal living (for example, the concept of free will).
now
Conwy Lloyd Morgan (1852-1936)
Argues that in explaining animal behavior one should not postulate faculties beyond those that are required to explain the behavior in question.
now
Emil Kraeplin (1856-1926)
Publishes a list of mental disorders in 1883 that was so thorough it was utilized worldwide until recent times.
now
Sigmund Freud (1856-1939)
Publishes Studies on Hysteria in 1895, thereby founding the school of psychoanalysis.
now
Alfred Binet (1857-1911)
Publishes the Binet-Simon scale of intelligence in 1905.
now
Vladimir M. Bechterev (1857-1927)
Argues that human behavior is reflexive and that it can be and should be studied and explained without reference to consciousness. Anticipates many of the features of Watsonian behaviorism
now
Edmund Husserl (1859-1938)
Proposes a pure phenomenology that describes all the mental processes available to humans in their efforts to understand the world.
now
John Dewey (1859-1952)
Publishes "The Reflex Arc in Psychology" in 1896, which is often viewed as marking the beginning of the school of functionalism.
now
James McKeen Cattell (1860-1944)
Uses the term "mental test" in 1890 and is a key figure in the school of functionalism and in the development of applied psychology
now
Hugo Münsterberg (1863-1916)
Replaces William James as director of the Harvard Psychology Laboratory in 1892. Makes significant contributions to such applied areas as clinical, forensic, and industrial psychology
now
Mary Whiton Calkins (1863-1930)
Does pioneering research on memory and creates an influential version of self psychology. In 1905 becomes the first woman president of the APA.
now
Charles Spearman (1863-1945)
Does pioneer work on the statistical technique later called factor analysis. Argues that intelligence consists of two factors: Specifc abilities (s) and general intelligence (g), and that g is mostly inherited.
now
Henry Herbert Goddard (1866-1957)
Translates the Binet-Simon scale into English but unlike Binet concludes that intelligence is largely inherited.
now
Edward Bradford Titchener (1867-1927)
Founds the school of structuralism that seeks to describe the basic elements of thought.
now
Lightner Witmer (1867-1956)
Establishes the first psychological clinic in 1896. In 1907 founds the journal The Psychological Clinic, and coins the term "clinical psychology" in its first issue.
now
Robert Sessions Woodworth (1869-1962)
A key functionalist at Columbia University, promotes dynamic psychology with an emphasis on motivation.
now
Alfred Adler (1870-1937)
Following the termination of his affliation with Freud, goes on to develop his own theory of personality featuring such concepts as feelings of inferiority, worldviews, fictional goals, lifestyles, and the creative self.
now
William Stern (1871-1938)
Introduces the term mental age (determined by performance on the Binet-Simon test) and suggests mental age (MA) be divided by chronological age (CA), yielding the intelligence quotient (IQ)
now
Margaret Floy Washburn (1871-1939)
In 1894 becomes the first woman to receive a PhD in psychology. In 1908 publishes The Animal Mind. In 1921 becomes the second woman president of the APA (Calkins was first).
now
Edward Lee Thorndike (1874-1949)
A transitional figure between functionalism and behaviorism. Experimentally studies trial and error learning and attempts to explain that learning without reference to consciousness.
now
Carl Jung (1875-1961)
Following the termination of his affliation with Freud in 1914, goes on to create his own theory of personality featuring powerful, inherited dispositions (archetypes)
now
Robert Yerkes (1876-1956)
Is largely responsible for creating the Army testing program during World War I and for supporting the argument that many of the nation's ills are caused by people of low intelligence.
now
Lewis Madison Terman (1877-1956)
Significantly modifes the Binet-Simon scale, thus creating the Stanford-Binet scale that was used to identify gifted children for further study.
now
John Broadus Watson (1878-1958)
Publishes "Psychology as a Behaviorist Views It" in 1913, thereby founding the school of behaviorism
now
Max Wertheimer (1880-1943)
Publishes "Experimental Studies of the Perception of Movement" in 1912, founding Gestalt psychology.
now
Ludwig Binswanger (1881-1966)
Integrates psychoanalytic theory and the writings of Husserl and Heidegger.
now
Clark Leonard Hull (1884-1952)
Creates a hypothetico-deductive theory of learning that he believes to be self-correcting. Most of the intervening variables in this theory are physiological.
now
Karen Horney (1885-1952)
Creates a version of psychoanalysis in which dysfunctional social relationships are seen as the causes of mental disorders instead of the intrapsyche conflict proposed by Freud.
now
Leta Stetter Hollingworth (1886- 1939)
Does pioneering work in the education of exceptional children. Her Gifted Children (1926) becomes a standard text in schools of education.
now
Edward Chace Tolman (1886-1959)
Publishes Purposive Behavior in Animals and Men, in which learning is explained primarily in terms of cognitive processes.
now
Edwin Ray Gunthrie (1886-1959)
Creates a highly parsimonious theory of learning that embraces the law of contiguity but rejects the law of frequency.
now
Frederick Charles Bartlett (1886-1969)
Publishes Remembering: A Study in Experimental and Social Psychology in 1932
now
Martin Heidegger (1889-1976)
Introduces into psychology such existential concepts as Dasein, authenticity, and thrownness.
now
Kurt Lewin (1890-1947)
Applies Gestalt principles to such topics as personality, motivation, conflict, and group dynamics.
now
Karl S. Lashley (1890-1958)
Publishes Brain Mechanisms and Intelligence in 1929
now
Percy W. Bridgman (1892-1961)
Publishes The Logic of Modern Physics in 1927, focusing on operational definitions.
now
Norbert Wiener (1894-1964)
Publishes Cybernetics in 1948.
now
Anna Freud (1895-1982)
Extends psychoanalytic principles to the treatment and understanding of children, makes significant contributions to the development of ego psychology, and becomes the official spokesperson for psychoanalysis following her father's death.
now
Jean Piaget (1896-1980)
In 1926 begins publishing an influential series of articles and books on intellectual development (genetic epistemology)
now
Carl Rogers (1902-1987)
Publishes Counseling and Psychotherapy: Newer Concepts in Practice in 1942. Creates a theory of personality featuring such concepts as the organismic valuing process, need for positive regard, conditions of worth, unconditional positive regard, and incongruency.
now
Karl Popper (1902-1994)
Publishes The Logic of Scientific Discovery in 1935.
now
Donald Hebb (1904-1985)
Publishes The Organization of Behavior in 1949, which describes his speculations about cell assemblies phase sequences and does much to promote cognitive and physiological psychology
now
Burrhus Frederic Skinner (1904-1990)
Proposes a system of thought that avoids theory by concentrating on how behavior is modified by its consequences. (Behaviourism)
now
George Kelly (1905-1967)
Creates largely existential theory of personality according to which people create to facilitate the accurate anticipation of future events; so-called mental disorders are actually perceptual construct systems problems.
now
Abraham Maslow (1908-1970)
Is usually considered the founder of humanistic (third-force) psychology. Creates a theory of personality with a hierarchy of needs such as physiological needs to self-actualization.
now
Rollo May (1909-1994)
Introduces existential philosophy and psychology into the U.S.
now
Roger Wolcott Sperry (1913-1994)
Conducted an influential series of experiments on hemispheric functioning using the split-brain preparation.
