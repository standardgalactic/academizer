Named after Hans Bethe, a notable physicist, it is especially useful when direct computation of the partition function is not tractable due to the complex interplay of interactions.The Bethe approximation makes use of factor graphs and exploits the locality of interactions. In a nutshell, it assumes that the system can be approximated as a tree-like structure, in which each particle/node only interacts with its nearest neighbors, and these interactions do not create any loops (circles). This greatly simplifies the calculation of the partition function.More technically, in the Bethe approximation, the joint distribution of states is factorized into the product of marginal probabilities over individual nodes and edges. The approximation can be viewed as a form of mean-field theory but with a more precise accounting of correlations between neighboring nodes. It is often used as an inference algorithm in probabilistic graphical models, particularly for approximate inference in Markov Random Fields or Bayesian Networks.Bethe approximation has been extensively applied in areas like statistical mechanics, combinatorics, computer science (particularly in artificial intelligence and machine learning), and information theory. LLaMA (Large Language Model Meta AI) is a large language model (LLM) released by Meta AI in February 2023. A variety of model sizes were trained ranging from 7 billion to 65 billion parameters. LLaMA's developers reported that the 13 billion parameter model's performance on most NLP benchmarks exceeded that of the much larger GPT-3 (with 175 billion parameters) and that the largest model was competitive with state of the art models such as PaLM and Chinchilla.[1] Whereas the most powerful LLMs have generally been accessible only through limited APIs (if at all), Meta released LLaMA's model weights to the research community under a noncommercial license.[2] Within a week of LLaMA's release, its weights were leaked to the public on 4chan via BitTorrent.[3]

LLaMA's developers focused their effort on scaling the model's performance by increasing the volume of training data, rather than the number of parameters, reasoning that the dominating cost for LLMs is from doing inference on the trained model rather than the computational cost of the training process.
