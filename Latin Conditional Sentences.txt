Indeed, in the field of grammar, the specific terms for the components of conditional sentences are "protasis" and "apodosis."
A protasis is the 'if' clause of a conditional sentence, which outlines the condition. For instance, in the sentences 'If he had the time', 'If I were rich', or 'If we won the match', the parts beginning with 'If' are the protases. They indicate the hypothetical scenario or condition that must be met for the consequence or outcome to occur.
On the other hand, the apodosis is the main clause or the 'then' clause in a conditional sentence, which illustrates the result or consequence of the condition. For instance, in the sentences 'He would have definitely come', 'I will buy a house', or 'We will celebrate', these portions are the apodoses. They convey what will (or would) happen assuming that the stipulated condition (the protasis) is (or were) fulfilled.
These terms are somewhat less commonly known outside of linguistic or academic contexts, but they offer precise ways to discuss the structure and function of conditional sentences.
