Jacques Ellul's "The Technological Society" is a seminal work in the philosophy of technology. Published in 1954, it presents a comprehensive critique of modern society's ever-increasing dependence on technology.
Ellul argues that technology, or "technique" as he calls it, has become autonomous and is evolving independently of human control. He suggests that it has pervaded every aspect of human life and society, including politics, economics, and culture.
"Technique," for Ellul, refers not just to machines, tools, or even technical methods, but to the totality of methods rationally arrived at and having absolute efficiency in every field of human activity. He perceives it as a mindset focused on rational efficiency and standardization, which values optimal results over moral or human considerations.
Ellul warns that the dominance of technique leads to "technological tyranny," undermining human freedom and creativity. He suggests that humans have become conditioned to think and behave in ways that serve the needs of technique, rather than technique serving human needs.
In terms of connections to our current discussion, Ellul's critique is particularly pertinent. The increasing reliance on AI and digital technologies in education, as discussed earlier, is a perfect example of the dominance of technique. While these tools can offer significant benefits, there's also a risk, as per Ellul's argument, of losing sight of human-centric learning values such as critical thinking, creativity, and social interaction.
Ellul's work invites us to consider how we can use technology in ways that truly serve human needs and enhance our capacities, rather than becoming unquestioningly subservient to the efficiency and standardization that technique demands. It's about achieving a balance where technology aids us without overriding the inherently human aspects of learning and communication.
