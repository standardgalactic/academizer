### Inner Model Theory
Inner model theory is a specialized area within set theory focused on constructing models of the universe of sets (denoted as \( V \)) that satisfy certain large cardinal axioms while being "inner" or more restricted than \( V \). These models help mathematicians understand the properties and implications of large cardinals by providing controlled environments to test hypotheses.

### Measurable Cardinal
A measurable cardinal is a type of large cardinal in set theory, characterized by the existence of a non-trivial, σ-additive measure defined on its subsets. This concept introduces higher levels of infinity and complexity into set theory, as measurable cardinals have properties that extend beyond those found in standard infinite sets like \( \aleph_0 \) (the cardinality of natural numbers).

Forcing is a method used in set theory to construct new models by adding specific sets. It was introduced by Paul Cohen to demonstrate the independence of certain mathematical propositions from Zermelo-Fraenkel set theory with the Axiom of Choice (ZFC). Through forcing, mathematicians can show that some statements are neither provable nor refutable using the standard axioms of set theory.

### Effectively Complete
A theory or axiom system is considered effectively complete if it can provide definitive answers to every question within its scope. In mathematical logic, this means having a decision procedure for determining the truth or falsity of any statement in the theory's language. However, Gödel's incompleteness theorems show that for sufficiently complex systems (like arithmetic), effective completeness is unattainable.

### Constructible Universe (L)
The constructible universe, denoted as \( L \), is a model of set theory introduced by Kurt Gödel. It consists of sets that can be explicitly constructed in a step-by-step process following specific rules. The constructible universe satisfies all the axioms of ZFC and provides a framework for exploring questions about definability and absoluteness in set theory.
