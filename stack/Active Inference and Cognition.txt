Active Inference can handle unpredictable environmental changes by continuously updating its generative model and beliefs about the environment based on the incoming sensory information. When the environment changes, the model can incorporate the new sensory input and update its predictions and beliefs about the current state of the environment. The model can also use its prior knowledge and expectations to guide its actions and adaptation to the new environment. The active inference agent can then adjust its behavior to minimize surprise and maintain homeostasis in the new environment. Active Inference can be applied to robotics or autonomous systems by providing a normative framework for designing and controlling these systems. Active Inference can be used to model the behavior of the system in response to sensory input, and to optimize the system's actions based on the expected outcomes and the level of surprise. Active Inference can also be used to design the sensory and motor interfaces of the system, and to specify the prior knowledge and expectations that guide the system's behavior. By using Active Inference, robotics and autonomous systems can adapt to changing environments, learn from experience, and optimize their behavior based on the principles of homeostasis and surprise minimization. Despite these limitations, Active Inference remains a promising framework for understanding and modeling cognition and behavior, and for designing intelligent systems that can adapt and learn in complex and dynamic environments.

Weak verbs
الفعل المعتلّ (al-fi3l al-mu3tall)

A verb is "weak" if one of the letters from the verb's three root letters is ا, و, or ي. There are three different classes of weak verbs; let's begin with:

Assimilated verbs
الفعل المثال (al-fi3l al-mitaal)
Verbs where the first radical is a long vowel (usually و).

Hollow verbs
الفعل الأجوف (al-fi3l al-ajwaf)
Verbs where the second radical is either a و (as with قال - يقول) or ي (as with باع - يبيع); in the perfect, the و or ي is replaced by an alif.

But the imperfect conjugation of سار saara is يسير yasiiru, so a short "i" would be used in those instances.

The third kind of weak verb is:

Defective verbs
الفعل الناقص (al-fi3l an-naaqiS)
Verbs where the final root radical is either a و or ي. There are three types of defective verbs:

Verbs with a و as the last root letter (ex. بدا - يبدو bada - yabdu, "to seem, appear")
Verbs with a ي as the last root letter and a kasra (short "i") as the middle vowel in the past/perfect tense, of the form "fa3iya - yaf3a" (ex. نسى - ينسى nasiya - yansa, "to forget")
Verbs with a ي as the last root letter and a fatHa (short "a") as the middle vowel in the past/perfect tense, of the form "fa3a - yaf3i" (ex. جرى - يجري jara - yajri, "to run")
Note that in Cairene Arabic, verbs of the type "fa3al" are often pronounced differently from standard Arabic in the past tense. While standard pronunciation would be "fa3al," Cairene pronunciation would be "fi3il." So "jara" becomes "giri."
