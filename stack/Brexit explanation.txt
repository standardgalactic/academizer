Brexit refers to the political process that led to the United Kingdom's (UK) withdrawal from the European Union (EU). The term "Brexit" is a portmanteau of "British" and "exit."
The Brexit process began in 2016 when the UK held a referendum to decide whether it should leave the EU or remain a member. The referendum resulted in a narrow majority of voters supporting Brexit, with 52% in favor of leaving the EU and 48% in favor of remaining.
Following the referendum, the UK government triggered Article 50 of the Treaty on European Union, which began the formal process of leaving the EU. Negotiations between the UK and the EU ensued, resulting in a withdrawal agreement in November 2018. The agreement included a transition period that lasted until December 31, 2020, during which time the UK continued to follow EU rules and regulations.
On January 31, 2020, the UK officially left the EU, and the transition period ended on December 31, 2020. The UK is now considered a third country by the EU, meaning that it no longer enjoys the same rights and privileges as it did as a member state. The UK and the EU continue to negotiate new arrangements covering trade, security, and other areas.

The Great Wall of China was built for several reasons, including:
Defense against invasions: One of the primary reasons for building the wall was to protect China from invasions by nomadic tribes from the north, such as the Mongols and the Manchus. The wall was built to serve as a barrier to prevent these tribes from entering China.
Trade control: Another reason for building the wall was to control the flow of trade along the Silk Road, which was an important trade route that connected China with the rest of the world. By building the wall, the Chinese authorities could regulate and tax the trade passing through the region.
