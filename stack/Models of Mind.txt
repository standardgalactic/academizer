Factors of Evaluation, Potency, and Activity
Osgood and his colleagues performed a factor analysis of large collections of semantic differential scales and found three recurring attitudes that people use to evaluate words and phrases: evaluation, potency, and activity. Evaluation loads highest on the adjective pair 'good-bad'. The 'strong-weak' adjective pair defines the potency factor. Adjective pair 'active-passive' defines the activity factor. These three dimensions of affective meaning were found to be cross-cultural universals in a study of dozens of cultures.

This factorial structure makes intuitive sense. When our ancestors encountered a person, the initial perception had to be whether that person represents a danger. Is the person good or bad? Next, is the person strong or weak? Our reactions to a person markedly differ if perceived as good and strong, good and weak, bad and weak, or bad and strong. Subsequently, we might extend our initial classification to include cases of persons who actively threaten us or represent only a potential danger, and so on. The evaluation, potency and activity factors thus encompass a detailed descriptive system of personality. Osgood's semantic differential measures these three factors. It contains sets of adjective pairs such as warm-cold, bright-dark, beautiful-ugly, sweet-bitter, fair-unfair, brave-cowardly, meaningful-meaningless.

The studies of Osgood and his colleagues revealed that the evaluative factor accounted for most of the variance in scalings, and related this to the idea of attitudes.

What about kahneman's later ideas on fast and slow thinking?
Kahneman's later work on fast and slow thinking refers to his distinction between two modes of thinking: System 1 and System 2. System 1 is intuitive, automatic, and effortless thinking, while System 2 is deliberative, controlled, and effortful thinking. According to Kahneman, most of our everyday decisions are made by System 1, while System 2 is used mainly for more complex tasks and decisions that require conscious attention and mental effort.
Kahneman argues that System 1 operates on a set of heuristics and biases that can lead to errors in judgment and decision-making, such as the anchoring bias, framing effects, and the availability heuristic. In contrast, System 2 is thought to be less prone to biases and more reflective, but is also more resource-intensive and therefore operates more slowly.
Kahneman's work on fast and slow thinking has had a significant impact on the field of psychology and has been widely cited and discussed in other disciplines, such as economics and decision science. It has led to a deeper understanding of how our cognitive biases and heuristics can shape our judgments and decisions and has inspired further research on how to correct for these biases and improve decision-making.
Relation to General Semantics?
Daniel Kahneman's work on fast and slow thinking and on the limitations of human judgment and decision-making has some similarities with the principles of General Semantics, which was developed by philosopher Alfred Korzybski in the early 20th century.
General Semantics is a field of study that explores the relationship between language, thought, and behavior, and emphasizes the importance of critical thinking and self-awareness in avoiding semantic and logical fallacies. Both Kahneman's work and General Semantics recognize the limitations of human thinking and the impact that language, emotions, and past experiences can have on judgment and decision-making.
However, General Semantics is a much broader field of study and encompasses many other ideas beyond the limitations of human thinking, while Kahneman's work is more focused on these limitations and the psychological processes that contribute to them. Nevertheless, both General Semantics and Kahneman's work can be useful in helping individuals to become more aware of the biases and limitations of their thinking and to improve their decision-making processes.

The History of the Bestiary:
In ancient times, scholars and naturalists created bestiaries, compendiums of animals and their symbolic meanings. These books were popular throughout the medieval world and were used to teach moral lessons, as well as to provide information on the natural world. The animals described within these volumes were often fantastical creatures, blending real and mythical traits to illustrate a particular point or moral.
One of the earliest and most famous examples of a bestiary is the Physiologus, a Greek work from the 2nd century AD that was widely translated and illustrated in the medieval period. It is said that this work influenced the development of the Christian bestiary, which used animal descriptions as allegories for religious teachings.
In the medieval period, bestiaries were highly prized and often illuminated with beautiful illustrations. They served as sources of wonder and entertainment, as well as educational tools for both young and old. Bestiaries remained popular until the Renaissance period, when the rise of scientific inquiry and the decline of feudalism led to a decline in their popularity. Today, bestiaries are valued as historical artifacts and as examples of medieval art and culture.
