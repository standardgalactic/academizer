What are some examples of crosstalking mechanisms between GABAergic and glutamatergic synaptic plasticity?
The primary mechanism for synaptic crosstalk during plasticity involves activity-dependent functioning of proteins in calcium-dependent signaling pathways. Examples of these proteins include voltage-gated calcium channels, the kinases CaMKII and PKC, the phosphatase calcineurin, and the protease calpain. However, there is still a lack of sufficient understanding of these mechanisms. (Page 2)
How does the balance between these two mechanisms affect overall brain function?
The balance between GABAergic and glutamatergic synaptic plasticity is critical for regulating neuronal activity by allowing neurons to adjust their synaptic strength in response to changes in activity. Increasing evidence indicates a coordination between GABAergic and glutamatergic synapses to maintain an optimal orchestrated balance of neuronal activity. (Page 2)

Define difficult terms
Sure, here are some definitions for the difficult terms mentioned in the PDF:

- Excitation and inhibition (E/I) balance: The balance between excitatory and inhibitory synaptic activity in the brain. This balance is important for regulating neuronal activity and maintaining proper brain function.
- Long-term potentiation (LTP): A process by which synaptic connections between neurons are strengthened. LTP is thought to be a cellular mechanism underlying learning and memory.
- Long-term depression (LTD): A process by which synaptic connections between neurons are weakened. LTD is thought to play a role in forgetting and unlearning.
- GABAAR: Gamma-aminobutyric acid type A receptor. This is a type of receptor that responds to the neurotransmitter GABA, which is an inhibitory neurotransmitter in the brain.
- NMDAR: N-methyl-D-aspartate receptor. This is a type of receptor that responds to the neurotransmitter glutamate, which is an excitatory neurotransmitter in the brain.
- Metabotropic GABABRs: A type of GABA receptor that works through a different mechanism than GABAARs. Metabotropic receptors activate intracellular signaling pathways when they bind to their ligand (in this case, GABA).
- Glutamatergic kainate and AMPA receptors: Two types of glutamate receptors that respond to different subtypes of glutamate. Kainate receptors are involved in modulating synaptic transmission, while AMPA receptors mediate fast excitatory synaptic transmission.
- Presynaptic signaling: Signaling events that occur at the presynaptic terminal (the end of an axon) before neurotransmitters are released into the synapse.
- Local translation: The process by which proteins are synthesized within dendrites or axons rather than at the cell body. Local translation allows for rapid changes in protein expression at specific synapses.
