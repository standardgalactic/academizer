 Holotype theory is a fundamental principle in biological taxonomy that involves designating a single, representative
specimen as the definitive reference for a newly described species. This specimen, known as the holotype, captures the
diagnostic characteristics of the new species and is deposited in a museum or institution for preservation and future
research. Alongside the holotype, researchers may also designate paratypes, which provide additional data on the
species' variability and distribution.

The holotype ensures nomenclatural stability by serving as a fixed reference for the species name, which is crucial when
questions about the species identity arise. In cases where multiple names have been proposed for the same species, the
International Code of Zoological Nomenclature (ICZN) dictates that the oldest available name with a holotype typically
takes priority.

Holotypes are critical for taxonomic research as they enable scientists to compare and classify specimens accurately.
However, they have limitations, such as not representing the full range of variation within a species and the risk of
loss or damage over time. Despite these challenges, holotype theory is essential for maintaining the integrity and
stability of scientific nomenclature in biology.
